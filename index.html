Mental Health Conditions

Understanding common mental health conditions, their symptoms, and available treatments

Anxiety Disorders

A group of mental health conditions characterized by excessive fear, worry, and related behavioral disturbances

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions, affecting millions of people worldwide. They can significantly impact daily life and functioning if left untreated.

Common Symptoms:

  • Excessive worry and fear
  • Restlessness and feeling on edge
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Physical symptoms (racing heart, sweating, trembling)

Types of Anxiety Disorders:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Panic Disorder
  • Social Anxiety Disorder
  • Specific Phobias

Depression

A common and serious medical illness that negatively affects how you feel, think, and act

Depression is more than just feeling sad or going through a rough patch. It's a serious mental health condition that requires understanding and medical care.

Common Symptoms:

  • Persistent sadness or emptiness
  • Loss of interest in activities once enjoyed
  • Changes in appetite and weight
  • Sleep disturbances (insomnia or oversleeping)
  • Fatigue and loss of energy
  • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt

Types of Depression:

  • Major Depressive Disorder
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder
  • Seasonal Affective Disorder
  • Postpartum Depression

Bipolar Disorder

A mental health condition characterized by extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression)

Bipolar disorder affects approximately 2.8% of adults in the U.S. and can significantly impact relationships, work, and daily life.

Common Symptoms:

  • Manic episodes (elevated mood, increased energy)
  • Depressive episodes (sadness, hopelessness)
  • Changes in sleep patterns
  • Impulsive or risky behavior
  • Difficulty concentrating

Types of Bipolar Disorder:

  • Bipolar I Disorder
  • Bipolar II Disorder
  • Cyclothymic Disorder

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

A mental health condition triggered by experiencing or witnessing a terrifying event

PTSD can develop after exposure to any event that results in psychological trauma, such as military combat, natural disasters, serious accidents, or personal assaults.

Common Symptoms:

  • Intrusive memories or flashbacks
  • Nightmares and sleep disturbances
  • Avoidance of trauma-related triggers
  • Negative changes in thinking and mood
  • Hypervigilance and exaggerated startle response

Treatment Approaches:

  • Trauma-focused therapy
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
  • Medication management

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity

ADHD affects both children and adults, though symptoms may present differently across age groups. Early diagnosis and treatment can significantly improve quality of life.

Common Symptoms:

  • Difficulty sustaining attention
  • Impulsivity and acting without thinking
  • Hyperactivity and restlessness
  • Poor time management
  • Difficulty organizing tasks

Types of ADHD:

  • Predominantly Inattentive Presentation
  • Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Presentation
  • Combined Presentation

Schizophrenia

A serious mental disorder characterized by a range of symptoms affecting thinking, behavior, and emotions

Schizophrenia affects about 1% of the population and typically appears in late adolescence or early adulthood. Early intervention and treatment are crucial for managing symptoms.

Common Symptoms:

  • Hallucinations (hearing or seeing things that aren't there)
  • Delusions (false beliefs)
  • Disorganized thinking and speech
  • Reduced emotional expression
  • Difficulty with daily functioning

Treatment Approaches:

  • Antipsychotic medications
  • Psychotherapy
  • Social skills training
  • Family support and education

Addictions

A complex condition characterized by compulsive substance use or behavior despite harmful consequences

Addiction can affect anyone and often requires comprehensive treatment approaches to address both the physical and psychological aspects of the condition.

Common Types:

  • Substance addiction (alcohol, drugs)
  • Behavioral addictions (gambling, internet)
  • Prescription medication addiction
  • Process addictions (shopping, eating)

Treatment Approaches:

  • Detoxification and medical management
  • Behavioral therapy
  • Support groups
  • Long-term aftercare planning

Common Questions About Mental Health

Find answers to frequently asked questions about mental health, therapy, and getting the support you need. If you don't see your question here, our team is always ready to help.

How do I know if I need mental health help?

Common signs that you might need mental health support include: persistent feelings of sadness or anxiety, significant changes in sleep or eating patterns, difficulty concentrating, loss of interest in activities you once enjoyed, or thoughts of self-harm. If these symptoms persist for more than two weeks or interfere with your daily life, it's important to seek professional help.

What's the difference between a psychiatrist and a psychologist?

Psychiatrists are medical doctors who can prescribe medication and specialize in the diagnosis and treatment of mental health disorders. Psychologists typically have a doctoral degree in psychology and focus on psychotherapy and behavioral interventions. While they cannot prescribe medication in most states, they provide valuable counseling and therapeutic services.

Will my insurance cover mental health treatment?

Under the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, most health insurance plans are required to provide coverage for mental health services. However, coverage details vary by plan. We recommend contacting your insurance provider directly to understand your specific coverage, including copays, deductibles, and any network restrictions.

What should I expect in my first therapy session?

Your first therapy session, often called an intake session, typically involves getting to know each other. The therapist will ask about your background, current concerns, and what you hope to achieve through therapy. You'll discuss treatment options and develop an initial plan. Remember, you can ask questions too, and it's important to feel comfortable with your therapist.

How long does therapy usually last?

The duration of therapy varies depending on your individual needs and goals. Some people benefit from short-term therapy (8-12 sessions) focused on specific issues, while others find long-term therapy more helpful for managing chronic conditions or exploring deeper patterns. Your therapist will work with you to determine the most appropriate treatment length.

Is everything I tell my therapist confidential?

Yes, with a few important exceptions. Therapists are legally required to break confidentiality if they believe you're in immediate danger of harming yourself or others, if they suspect abuse of a minor or elderly person, or if they're subpoenaed by a court. Otherwise, your information is protected by strict privacy laws and ethical guidelines.
